					<div id="mw-content-text" class="mw-body-content"><div class="mw-subjectpageheader">
</div><div class="mw-content-ltr mw-parser-output" lang="en" dir="ltr"><div class="shortdescription nomobile noexcerpt noprint searchaux" style="display:none">Scientific theory</div>
<p><b>Higher-order theories of consciousness</b> postulate that <a href="/wiki/Consciousness" title="Consciousness">consciousness</a> consists in <a href="/wiki/Perceptions" class="mw-redirect" title="Perceptions">perceptions</a> or thoughts about first-order <a href="/wiki/Mental_states" class="mw-redirect" title="Mental states">mental states</a>.<sup id="cite&#95;ref-Carruthers2011&#95;1-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Carruthers2011-1"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>1<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup><sup id="cite&#95;ref-RosenthalWeisberg2008&#95;2-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-RosenthalWeisberg2008-2"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>2<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup><sup id="cite&#95;ref-Droege&#95;3-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Droege-3"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>3<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> In particular, <a href="/wiki/Phenomenal_consciousness" class="mw-redirect" title="Phenomenal consciousness">phenomenal consciousness</a> is thought to be a higher-order <a href="/wiki/Mental_representation" title="Mental representation">representation</a> of perceptual or quasi-perceptual contents, such as visual images.<sup id="cite&#95;ref-Carruthers2011&#95;1-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Carruthers2011-1"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>1<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup>
</p><p>Higher-order theories are distinguished from other <a href="/wiki/Cognition" title="Cognition">cognitive</a>/representational accounts of consciousness which suggest that merely first-order mentality of certain sorts constitutes consciousness.<sup id="cite&#95;ref-Carruthers2011&#95;1-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Carruthers2011-1"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>1<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup>
</p>
<meta property="mw:PageProp/toc" />
<div class="mw-heading mw-heading2"><h2 id="Types">Types</h2><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Higher-order_theories_of_consciousness&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Types"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></div>
<div class="mw-heading mw-heading3"><h3 id="Higher-order_perception_theory">Higher-order perception theory</h3><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Higher-order_theories_of_consciousness&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Higher-order perception theory"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></div>
<p>Also called <b>inner-sense theory</b>, this version of higher-order theory proposes that phenomenal consciousness consists not in immediate sensations but in higher-level sensing of those <a href="/wiki/Sensation_(psychology)" class="mw-redirect" title="Sensation (psychology)">sensations</a>. Or put another way:
</p>
<dl><dd>A phenomenally conscious mental state is a state with analog/non-conceptual <a href="/wiki/Intentionality" title="Intentionality">intentional</a> content, which is in turn the target of a higher-order analog/non-conceptual intentional state, via the operations of a faculty of 'inner sense'.<sup id="cite&#95;ref-Carruthers2011&#95;1-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Carruthers2011-1"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>1<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup></dd></dl>
<p>One motivation for this approach is that it accounts for phenomenal consciousness absent beliefs or behaviors associated with those experiences—so that, e.g., someone could feel pain without necessarily exhibiting functional reactions to pain.<sup id="cite&#95;ref-Carruthers2011&#95;1-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Carruthers2011-1"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>1<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup>
</p>
<div class="mw-heading mw-heading3"><h3 id="Actualist_higher-order_thought_theory">Actualist higher-order thought theory</h3><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Higher-order_theories_of_consciousness&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Actualist higher-order thought theory"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></div>
<p><a href="/wiki/David_M._Rosenthal_(philosopher)" title="David M. Rosenthal (philosopher)">David Rosenthal</a> is a foremost advocate of this view. It claims that a mental state is conscious when it is the subject of a <b>higher-order thought</b> (HOT). Phenomenal consciousness in particular corresponds to certain kinds of mental states (e.g., visual inputs) that are the subjects of HOTs. Rosenthal excludes the special case in which one learns about one's lower-order states by conscious deduction. For instance, if <a href="/wiki/Psychoanalysis" title="Psychoanalysis">psychoanalysis</a> could reveal one's unconscious motives, this would not suddenly make them conscious.<sup id="cite&#95;ref-Carruthers2011&#95;1-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Carruthers2011-1"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>1<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup>
</p>
<div class="mw-heading mw-heading3"><h3 id="Dispositionalist_higher-order_thought_theory">Dispositionalist higher-order thought theory</h3><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Higher-order_theories_of_consciousness&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Dispositionalist higher-order thought theory"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></div>
<p>The dispositionalist mirrors the actualist view except that the first-order mental state need not actually be thought about—it only needs to be available to potentially be thought about.<sup id="cite&#95;ref-Carruthers2011&#95;1-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Carruthers2011-1"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>1<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup>
</p><p>While actualist accounts would seem to require immense higher-order computation on all first-order percepts, dispositionalist accounts do not; they merely require <i>availability</i> of first-order information. Such availability could come from, e.g., global broadcasting as in the <a href="/wiki/Global_Workspace_Theory" class="mw-redirect" title="Global Workspace Theory">Global Workspace Theory</a>.<sup id="cite&#95;ref-Carruthers2011&#95;1-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Carruthers2011-1"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>1<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup>
</p>
<div class="mw-heading mw-heading3"><h3 id="Self-representational_higher-order_theories">Self-representational higher-order theories</h3><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Higher-order_theories_of_consciousness&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Self-representational higher-order theories"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></div>
<p>Self-representational higher-order theories consider the higher-order state to be <i>constitutive</i> or <i>internal</i> to its first-order state.<sup id="cite&#95;ref-Carruthers2011&#95;1-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Carruthers2011-1"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>1<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> This may be either because:
</p>
<ol><li>the first-order and higher-order states are identical, with the same state serving two different roles, or</li>
<li>the first-order and higher-order states are part of the same whole, and the whole complex is what becomes conscious.<sup id="cite&#95;ref-Carruthers2011&#95;1-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Carruthers2011-1"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>1<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup></li></ol>
<p>An example of the second, "part-whole" self-representational theory is Vincent Picciuto's "quotational theory of consciousness" in which consciousness consists of "mentally quoting" a first-order perception.<sup id="cite&#95;ref-Picciuto2013&#95;4-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Picciuto2013-4"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>4<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup>
</p>
<div class="mw-heading mw-heading3"><h3 id="Higher-order_statistical_inference_view">Higher-order statistical inference view</h3><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Higher-order_theories_of_consciousness&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Higher-order statistical inference view"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></div>
<p>In this theory, higher-level processing determines that a first-order representation is reliable.<sup id="cite&#95;ref-LauRosenthal2011&#95;5-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-LauRosenthal2011-5"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>5<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup><sup class="reference nowrap"><span title="Page / location: 366">&#58;&#8202;366&#8202;</span></sup>
</p>
<div class="mw-heading mw-heading3"><h3 id="Radical_plasticity_thesis">Radical plasticity thesis</h3><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Higher-order_theories_of_consciousness&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Radical plasticity thesis"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></div>
<p>Similar to a HOT view, this theory proposes that the brain "learns" when there exists a trustworthy lower-level representation.<sup id="cite&#95;ref-LauRosenthal2011&#95;5-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-LauRosenthal2011-5"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>5<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup><sup class="reference nowrap"><span title="Page / location: 366">&#58;&#8202;366&#8202;</span></sup>
</p>
<div class="mw-heading mw-heading2"><h2 id="Scientific_perspectives">Scientific perspectives</h2><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Higher-order_theories_of_consciousness&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Scientific perspectives"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></div>
<p>Higher-order theories originated in philosophy, but they have also gained some scientific defenders.<sup id="cite&#95;ref-LauRosenthal2011&#95;5-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-LauRosenthal2011-5"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>5<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup><sup class="reference nowrap"><span title="Page / location: 365">&#58;&#8202;365&#8202;</span></sup> Here are some examples of evidence supporting higher-order views:
</p>
<ul><li>Reporting of conscious experience seems in some studies to occur serially following unconscious processing rather than in parallel with it.<sup id="cite&#95;ref-LauRosenthal2011&#95;5-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-LauRosenthal2011-5"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>5<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup><sup class="reference nowrap"><span title="Page / location: 368">&#58;&#8202;368&#8202;</span></sup></li>
<li>Unconscious processing is quite powerful on its own, so it is not obvious that task performance requires consciousness. Higher-order views agree with this, unlike, e.g., global-workspace views.<sup id="cite&#95;ref-LauRosenthal2011&#95;5-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-LauRosenthal2011-5"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>5<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup><sup class="reference nowrap"><span title="Page / location: 368">&#58;&#8202;368&#8202;</span></sup></li>
<li>Impairments to the prefrontal cortex may impair subjective reports without affecting task performance. If the prefrontal cortex were mainly serving an attentional role, performance should degrade along with reportability.<sup id="cite&#95;ref-LauRosenthal2011&#95;5-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-LauRosenthal2011-5"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>5<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup><sup class="reference nowrap"><span title="Page / location: 369">&#58;&#8202;369&#8202;</span></sup></li>
<li>Some interpretations of certain disorders of consciousness suggest that they operate by affecting the prefrontal cortex, where higher-order thoughts are assumed to take place.<sup id="cite&#95;ref-LauRosenthal2011&#95;5-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-LauRosenthal2011-5"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>5<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup><sup class="reference nowrap"><span title="Page / location: 370–71">&#58;&#8202;370–71&#8202;</span></sup></li></ul>
<p><a href="/wiki/Edmund_Rolls" title="Edmund Rolls">Edmund Rolls</a> defends a higher-order account of consciousness. He argues that consciousness consists in higher-order thoughts allowing one to monitor and correct errors and "that the brain systems that are required for consciousness and language are similar."<sup id="cite&#95;ref-Rolls2005&#95;6-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Rolls2005-6"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>6<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup><sup class="reference nowrap"><span title="Page / location: 404–05">&#58;&#8202;404–05&#8202;</span></sup> <a href="/wiki/Qualia" title="Qualia">Qualia</a> like pain become conscious when "they enter into a specialized linguistic symbol-manipulation system, which is part of a higher-order thought system" that helps with, inter alia, "flexible planning of actions."<sup id="cite&#95;ref-Rolls2005&#95;6-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Rolls2005-6"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>6<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup><sup class="reference nowrap"><span title="Page / location: 407">&#58;&#8202;407&#8202;</span></sup>
</p>
<div class="mw-heading mw-heading2"><h2 id="Motivation">Motivation</h2><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Higher-order_theories_of_consciousness&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Motivation"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></div>
<p>Higher-order theory can account for the distinction between unconscious and conscious brain processing. Both types of mental operations involve first-order manipulations, and according to higher-order theory, what makes cognition conscious is a higher-order observation of the first-order processing.<sup id="cite&#95;ref-Carruthers2011&#95;1-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Carruthers2011-1"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>1<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup>
</p><p>In <a href="/wiki/Neuroscience" title="Neuroscience">neuroscience</a> terms, higher-order theory is motivated by the distinction between first-order information in early sensory regions versus higher-order representations in <a href="/wiki/Prefrontal_cortex" title="Prefrontal cortex">prefrontal</a> and <a href="/wiki/Parietal_lobe" title="Parietal lobe">parietal cortices</a>.<sup id="cite&#95;ref-LauRosenthal2011&#95;5-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-LauRosenthal2011-5"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>5<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup><sup class="reference nowrap"><span title="Page / location: 365–66">&#58;&#8202;365–66&#8202;</span></sup>
</p>
<div class="mw-heading mw-heading2"><h2 id="Criticisms">Criticisms</h2><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Higher-order_theories_of_consciousness&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Criticisms"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></div>
<div class="mw-heading mw-heading3"><h3 id="Against_inner-sense_and_actualist_HOT_views">Against inner-sense and actualist HOT views</h3><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Higher-order_theories_of_consciousness&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Against inner-sense and actualist HOT views"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></div>
<p>Scott Sturgeon argues against inner-sense theory on the grounds that it could give rise to disorders in which, e.g., one has a first-order perception of red which mis-triggers a second-order sense of "looks orange". But we do not see any such disorders in <a href="/wiki/Neurology" title="Neurology">neurology</a>.<sup id="cite&#95;ref-Carruthers2011&#95;1-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Carruthers2011-1"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>1<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup><sup id="cite&#95;ref-Sturgeon2000&#95;7-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Sturgeon2000-7"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>7<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> More generally, inner-sense and actualist views face the "targetless higher-order representation problem" in which there might be, e.g., a higher-order experience/thought about perceiving red without a corresponding first-order redness percept.<sup id="cite&#95;ref-Carruthers2011&#95;1-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Carruthers2011-1"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>1<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> One reply is that this is no more a problem for higher-order theories than for other neuroscientific theories of consciousness, which also involve many layers of processing that could theoretically be inconsistent.<sup id="cite&#95;ref-LauRosenthal2011&#95;5-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-LauRosenthal2011-5"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>5<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup><sup class="reference nowrap"><span title="Page / location: 370">&#58;&#8202;370&#8202;</span></sup>
</p><p><a href="/wiki/Peter_Carruthers_(philosopher)" title="Peter Carruthers (philosopher)">Peter Carruthers</a> points out that inner sense of or actualist HOTs about first-order percepts might greatly increase the computing power required to consciously process stimuli, for not only does one need to have a perception, but one then needs to have another (perhaps highly detailed) perception or thought about that perception.<sup id="cite&#95;ref-Carruthers2011&#95;1-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Carruthers2011-1"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>1<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup><sup id="cite&#95;ref-Carruthers2000&#95;8-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Carruthers2000-8"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>8<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup>
</p>
