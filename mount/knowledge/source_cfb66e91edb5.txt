					<div id="mw-content-text" class="mw-body-content"><div class="mw-subjectpageheader">
</div><div class="mw-content-ltr mw-parser-output" lang="en" dir="ltr"><div class="shortdescription nomobile noexcerpt noprint searchaux" style="display:none">Field of study</div>
<style data-mw-deduplicate="TemplateStyles:r1320445320">.mw-parser-output .hatnote{font-style:italic}.mw-parser-output div.hatnote{padding-left:1.6em;margin-bottom:0.5em}.mw-parser-output .hatnote i{font-style:normal}.mw-parser-output .hatnote+link+.hatnote{margin-top:-0.5em}@media print{body.ns-0 .mw-parser-output .hatnote{display:none!important}}</style><div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">This article is about a field of research. For other uses, see <a href="/wiki/Artificial_life_(disambiguation)" class="mw-disambig" title="Artificial life (disambiguation)">Artificial life (disambiguation)</a>.</div>
<link rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style" href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r1320445320" /><div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">"ALife" redirects here. For other uses, see <a href="/wiki/Alife_(disambiguation)" class="mw-redirect mw-disambig" title="Alife (disambiguation)">Alife</a>.</div>
<figure class="mw-default-size" typeof="mw:File/Thumb"><a href="/wiki/File:Unevolved_swimbots_showing_genetic_diversity.png" class="mw-file-description"><img src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/48/Unevolved_swimbots_showing_genetic_diversity.png/250px-Unevolved_swimbots_showing_genetic_diversity.png" decoding="async" width="250" height="250" class="mw-file-element" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/48/Unevolved_swimbots_showing_genetic_diversity.png/500px-Unevolved_swimbots_showing_genetic_diversity.png 1.5x" data-file-width="1000" data-file-height="1000" /></a><figcaption>A selection of simulated "swimbots"</figcaption></figure>
<p><b>Artificial Life</b>, also referred to as <b>ALife</b> or <b>A-Life</b>, is a field of study wherein researchers examine <a href="/wiki/System" title="System">systems</a> related to natural <a href="/wiki/Life" title="Life">life</a>, its processes, and its evolution, through the use of <a href="/wiki/Simulation" title="Simulation">simulations</a> with <a href="/wiki/Computer_models" class="mw-redirect" title="Computer models">computer models</a>, <a href="/wiki/Robotics" title="Robotics">robotics</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Biochemistry" title="Biochemistry">biochemistry</a>.<sup id="cite&#95;ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>1<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup><sup id="cite&#95;ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>2<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> The discipline was named by <a href="/wiki/Christopher_Langton" title="Christopher Langton">Christopher Langton</a>, an American <a href="/wiki/Computer_scientist" title="Computer scientist">computer scientist</a>, in 1986.<sup id="cite&#95;ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>3<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> In 1987, Langton organized the first conference on the field, in <a href="/wiki/Los_Alamos,_New_Mexico" title="Los Alamos, New Mexico">Los Alamos, New Mexico</a>.<sup id="cite&#95;ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>4<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> There are three main kinds of artificial life,<sup id="cite&#95;ref-Mark2003&#95;5-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Mark2003-5"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>5<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> named for their approaches: <i>soft</i>,<sup id="cite&#95;ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>6<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> from <a href="/wiki/Software" title="Software">software</a>; <i>hard</i>,<sup id="cite&#95;ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>7<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> from <a href="/wiki/Computer_hardware" title="Computer hardware">hardware</a>; and <i><a href="/wiki/Wet_artificial_life" class="mw-redirect" title="Wet artificial life">wet</a></i>, from biochemistry. Artificial life researchers study traditional <a href="/wiki/Biology" title="Biology">biology</a> by trying to replicate aspects of biological phenomena.<sup id="cite&#95;ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>8<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup><sup id="cite&#95;ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>9<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup>
</p>
<meta property="mw:PageProp/toc" />
<div class="mw-heading mw-heading2"><h2 id="Overview">Overview</h2><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Artificial_life&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Overview"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></div>
<p>Artificial life studies the fundamental processes of <a href="/wiki/Living_system" class="mw-redirect" title="Living system">living systems</a> in artificial environments in order to gain a deeper understanding of the complex information processing that defines such systems.  These topics are broad, but often include <a href="/wiki/Evolutionary_algorithm" title="Evolutionary algorithm">evolutionary dynamics</a>, <a href="/wiki/Emergence" title="Emergence">emergent properties</a> of collective systems, <a href="/wiki/Biomimicry" class="mw-redirect" title="Biomimicry">biomimicry</a>, as well as related issues about the <a href="/wiki/Philosophy_of_biology" title="Philosophy of biology">philosophy of the nature of life</a> and the use of lifelike properties in artistic works.<sup id="cite&#95;ref-Mark2003&#95;5-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Mark2003-5"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>5<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup>
</p>
<div class="mw-heading mw-heading2"><h2 id="Philosophy">Philosophy</h2><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Artificial_life&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Philosophy"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></div>
<p>The modeling philosophy of artificial life strongly differs from traditional modeling by studying not only "life as we know it" but also "life as it could be".<sup id="cite&#95;ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>10<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup>
</p><p>A traditional model of a biological system will focus on capturing its most important parameters. In contrast, an alife modeling approach will generally seek to decipher the most simple and general principles underlying life and implement them in a simulation. The simulation then offers the possibility to analyse new and different lifelike systems.
</p><p>Vladimir Georgievich Red'ko proposed to generalize this distinction to the modeling of any process, leading to the more general distinction of "processes as we know them" and "processes as they could be".<sup id="cite&#95;ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>11<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup>
</p><p>At present, the commonly accepted <a href="/wiki/Life#Definitions" title="Life">definition of life</a> does not consider any current alife simulations or <a href="/wiki/Software" title="Software">software</a> to be alive, and they do not constitute part of the evolutionary process of any <a href="/wiki/Ecosystem" title="Ecosystem">ecosystem</a>. However, different opinions about artificial life's potential have arisen:
</p>
<ul><li>The <i>strong alife</i> (cf. <a href="/wiki/Philosophy_of_artificial_intelligence#Strong_AI_vs._weak_AI" title="Philosophy of artificial intelligence">Strong AI</a>)  position states that "life is a process which can be abstracted away from any particular medium" (John von Neumann).<sup id="cite&#95;ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>12<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> This view is rooted in von Neumann's work on <b>cellular automata</b> and <b>universal constructors</b>, which demonstrated that self-reproduction could be achieved by logic-based machines regardless of their physical substrate. Notably, <a href="/wiki/Thomas_S._Ray" title="Thomas S. Ray">Tom Ray</a> declared that his program <a href="/wiki/Tierra_(computer_simulation)" title="Tierra (computer simulation)">Tierra</a> is not simulating life in a computer but synthesizing it.<sup id="cite&#95;ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>13<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup></li>
<li>The <i>weak alife</i> position denies the possibility of generating a "living process" outside of a chemical solution. Its researchers try instead to simulate life processes to understand the underlying mechanics of biological phenomena.</li></ul>
<p>A central goal in the philosophy and modeling of artificial life is achieving <b>Open-Ended Evolution (OEE)</b>. This refers to the capacity of a system to continually produce novel, complex, and adaptive behaviors or entities without reaching a stable equilibrium or predefined end-point.<sup id="cite&#95;ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>14<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> Researchers argue that OEE is a hallmark of natural life that current artificial systems have yet to fully replicate.<sup id="cite&#95;ref-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-15"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>15<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup>
</p>
<div class="mw-heading mw-heading2"><h2 id="Software-based_(&quot;soft&quot;)"><span id="Software-based_.28.22soft.22.29"></span>Software-based ("soft")</h2><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Artificial_life&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Software-based (&quot;soft&quot;)"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></div>
<div class="mw-heading mw-heading3"><h3 id="Techniques">Techniques</h3><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Artificial_life&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Techniques"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></div>
<ul><li><a href="/wiki/Cellular_automaton" title="Cellular automaton">Cellular automata</a> were used in the early days of artificial life, and are still often used for ease of <a href="/wiki/Scalability" title="Scalability">scalability</a> and <a href="/wiki/Parallelization" class="mw-redirect" title="Parallelization">parallelization</a>.  Alife and cellular automata share a closely tied history.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Artificial_neural_network" class="mw-redirect" title="Artificial neural network">Artificial neural networks</a> are sometimes used to model the brain of an agent.  Although traditionally more of an <a href="/wiki/Artificial_intelligence" title="Artificial intelligence">artificial intelligence</a> technique, neural nets can be important for simulating <a href="/wiki/Population_dynamics" title="Population dynamics">population dynamics</a> of organisms that can <i>learn</i>.  The symbiosis between learning and evolution is central to theories about the development of instincts in organisms with higher neurological complexity, as in, for instance, the <a href="/wiki/Baldwin_effect" title="Baldwin effect">Baldwin effect</a>.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Neuroevolution" title="Neuroevolution">Neuroevolution</a></li></ul>
<div class="mw-heading mw-heading4"><h4 id="Program-based">Program-based</h4><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Artificial_life&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Program-based"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></div>
<link rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style" href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r1320445320" /><div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Further information: <a href="/wiki/Programming_game" title="Programming game">programming game</a></div>
<p>Program-based simulations contain organisms with a "genome" language.  This language is more often in the form of a <a href="/wiki/Turing_complete" class="mw-redirect" title="Turing complete">Turing complete</a> computer program than actual biological DNA.  Assembly derivatives are the most common languages used. An organism "lives" when its code is executed, and there are usually various methods allowing <a href="/wiki/Self-replication" title="Self-replication">self-replication</a>. Mutations are generally implemented as random changes to the code. Use of <a href="/wiki/Cellular_automata" class="mw-redirect" title="Cellular automata">cellular automata</a> is common but not required. Another example could be an <a href="/wiki/Artificial_intelligence" title="Artificial intelligence">artificial intelligence</a> and <a href="/wiki/Multi-agent_system" title="Multi-agent system">multi-agent system/program</a>.
</p>
<div class="mw-heading mw-heading4"><h4 id="Module-based">Module-based</h4><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Artificial_life&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Module-based"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></div>
<figure class="mw-default-size mw-halign-right" typeof="mw:File/Thumb"><a href="/wiki/File:Braitenberg_vehicle_(simulation_made_with_breve).jpg" class="mw-file-description"><img src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c5/Braitenberg_vehicle_%28simulation_made_with_breve%29.jpg/250px-Braitenberg_vehicle_%28simulation_made_with_breve%29.jpg" decoding="async" width="250" height="250" class="mw-file-element" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c5/Braitenberg_vehicle_%28simulation_made_with_breve%29.jpg/400px-Braitenberg_vehicle_%28simulation_made_with_breve%29.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/c/c5/Braitenberg_vehicle_%28simulation_made_with_breve%29.jpg 2x" data-file-width="400" data-file-height="400" /></a><figcaption>A <a href="/wiki/Braitenberg_vehicle" title="Braitenberg vehicle">Braitenberg vehicle</a>, able to navigate by light detection</figcaption></figure>
<p>Individual modules are added to a creature.  These modules modify the creature's behaviors and characteristics either directly, by hard coding into the simulation (leg type A increases speed and metabolism), or indirectly, through the emergent interactions between a creature's modules (leg type A moves up and down with a frequency of X, which interacts with other legs to create motion).  Generally, these are simulators that emphasize user creation and accessibility over mutation and evolution.
</p>
<div class="mw-heading mw-heading4"><h4 id="Parameter-based">Parameter-based</h4><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Artificial_life&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Parameter-based"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></div>
<p>Organisms are generally constructed with pre-defined and fixed behaviors that are controlled by various parameters that mutate.  That is, each organism contains a collection of numbers or other <i>finite</i> parameters. Each parameter controls one or several aspects of an organism in a well-defined way.
</p>
<div class="mw-heading mw-heading4"><h4 id="Neural_net–based"><span id="Neural_net.E2.80.93based"></span>Neural net–based</h4><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Artificial_life&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Neural net–based"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></div>
<p>These simulations have creatures that learn and grow using neural nets or a close derivative.  Emphasis is often, although not always, on learning rather than on natural selection.
</p>
<div class="mw-heading mw-heading3"><h3 id="Complex_systems_modeling">Complex systems modeling</h3><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Artificial_life&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Complex systems modeling"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></div><p>
Mathematical models of complex systems are of three types: <a href="/wiki/Black-box" class="mw-redirect" title="Black-box">black-box</a> (phenomenological), <a href="/wiki/White_box_(software_engineering)" title="White box (software engineering)">white-box</a> (mechanistic, based on the <a href="/wiki/First_principles" class="mw-redirect" title="First principles">first principles</a>) and <a href="/wiki/Grey_box_model" title="Grey box model">grey-box</a> (mixtures of phenomenological and mechanistic models).<sup id="cite&#95;ref-Kalmykov&#95;Lev&#95;V.,&#95;Kalmykov&#95;Vyacheslav&#95;L.&#95;Solution&#95;16-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Kalmykov_Lev_V.,_Kalmykov_Vyacheslav_L._Solution-16"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>16<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup><sup id="cite&#95;ref-Kalmykov&#95;Lev&#95;V.,&#95;Kalmykov&#95;Vyacheslav&#95;L.&#95;White-box&#95;model&#95;17-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Kalmykov_Lev_V.,_Kalmykov_Vyacheslav_L._White-box_model-17"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>17<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> In black-box models, the individual-based (mechanistic) mechanisms of a complex dynamic system remain hidden. </p><figure class="mw-default-size" typeof="mw:File/Thumb"><a href="/wiki/File:Mathematical_models_for_complex_systems.jpg" class="mw-file-description"><img src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f3/Mathematical_models_for_complex_systems.jpg/250px-Mathematical_models_for_complex_systems.jpg" decoding="async" width="250" height="137" class="mw-file-element" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f3/Mathematical_models_for_complex_systems.jpg/500px-Mathematical_models_for_complex_systems.jpg 1.5x" data-file-width="2100" data-file-height="1150" /></a><figcaption>Mathematical models for complex systems</figcaption></figure><p> Black-box models are completely nonmechanistic. They are phenomenological and ignore a composition and internal structure of a complex system. Due to the non-transparent nature of the model, interactions of subsystems cannot be investigated. In contrast, a white-box model of a complex dynamic system has ‘transparent walls’ and directly shows underlying mechanisms. All events at the micro-, meso- and macro-levels of a dynamic system are directly visible at all stages of a white-box model's evolution. In most cases, mathematical modelers use the heavy black-box mathematical methods, which cannot produce mechanistic models of complex dynamic systems. Grey-box models are intermediate and combine black-box and white-box approaches. </p><figure class="mw-default-size" typeof="mw:File/Thumb"><a href="/wiki/File:Logical_deterministic_individual-based_cellular_automata_model_of_single_species_population_growth.gif" class="mw-file-description"><img src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/bf/Logical_deterministic_individual-based_cellular_automata_model_of_single_species_population_growth.gif/250px-Logical_deterministic_individual-based_cellular_automata_model_of_single_species_population_growth.gif" decoding="async" width="250" height="258" class="mw-file-element" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/bf/Logical_deterministic_individual-based_cellular_automata_model_of_single_species_population_growth.gif/500px-Logical_deterministic_individual-based_cellular_automata_model_of_single_species_population_growth.gif 1.5x" data-file-width="835" data-file-height="861" /></a><figcaption>Logical deterministic individual-based cellular automata model of single species population growth</figcaption></figure><p> Creation of a white-box model of complex system is associated with the problem of the necessity of an a priori basic knowledge of the modeling subject. The deterministic logical <a href="/wiki/Cellular_automaton" title="Cellular automaton">cellular automata</a> are necessary but not sufficient condition of a white-box model. The second necessary prerequisite of a white-box model is the presence of the physical <a href="/wiki/Ontology" title="Ontology">ontology</a> of the object under study. The white-box modeling represents an automatic hyper-logical inference from the <a href="/wiki/First_principle" title="First principle">first principles</a> because it is completely based on the deterministic logic and axiomatic theory of the subject. The purpose of the white-box modeling is to derive from the basic axioms a more detailed, more concrete mechanistic knowledge about the dynamics of the object under study. The necessity to formulate an intrinsic <a href="/wiki/Axiomatic_system" title="Axiomatic system">axiomatic system</a> of the subject before creating its white-box model distinguishes the cellular automata models of white-box type from cellular automata models based on arbitrary logical rules. If cellular automata rules have not been formulated from the first principles of the subject, then such a model may have a weak relevance to the real problem.<sup id="cite&#95;ref-Kalmykov&#95;Lev&#95;V.,&#95;Kalmykov&#95;Vyacheslav&#95;L.&#95;White-box&#95;model&#95;17-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Kalmykov_Lev_V.,_Kalmykov_Vyacheslav_L._White-box_model-17"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>17<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup>
</p><figure class="mw-default-size" typeof="mw:File/Thumb"><a href="/wiki/File:Logical_deterministic_individual-based_cellular_automata_model_of_interspecific_competition_for_a_single_limited_resource.gif" class="mw-file-description"><img src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/10/Logical_deterministic_individual-based_cellular_automata_model_of_interspecific_competition_for_a_single_limited_resource.gif/250px-Logical_deterministic_individual-based_cellular_automata_model_of_interspecific_competition_for_a_single_limited_resource.gif" decoding="async" width="250" height="296" class="mw-file-element" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/10/Logical_deterministic_individual-based_cellular_automata_model_of_interspecific_competition_for_a_single_limited_resource.gif/500px-Logical_deterministic_individual-based_cellular_automata_model_of_interspecific_competition_for_a_single_limited_resource.gif 1.5x" data-file-width="687" data-file-height="814" /></a><figcaption>Logical deterministic individual-based cellular automata model of interspecific competition for a single limited resource</figcaption></figure>

<div class="mw-heading mw-heading2"><h2 id="Hardware-based_(&quot;hard&quot;)"><span id="Hardware-based_.28.22hard.22.29"></span>Hardware-based ("hard")</h2><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Artificial_life&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Hardware-based (&quot;hard&quot;)"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></div>
<link rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style" href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r1320445320" /><div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Further information: <a href="/wiki/Robot" title="Robot">Robot</a></div>
<p>Hardware-based artificial life mainly consist of <i>robots</i>, that is, <a href="/wiki/Automation" title="Automation">automatically</a> guided <a href="/wiki/Machine" title="Machine">machines</a> able to do tasks on their own.
</p>
<div class="mw-heading mw-heading2"><h2 id="Biochemical-based_(&quot;wet&quot;)"><span id="Biochemical-based_.28.22wet.22.29"></span>Biochemical-based ("wet")</h2><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Artificial_life&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Biochemical-based (&quot;wet&quot;)"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></div>
<link rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style" href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r1320445320" /><div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Further information: <a href="/wiki/Artificial_cell" title="Artificial cell">Artificial cell</a>, <a href="/wiki/Synthetic_biology" title="Synthetic biology">Synthetic biology</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Xenobiology" title="Xenobiology">Xenobiology</a></div>
<p>Biochemical-based life is studied in the field of <a href="/wiki/Synthetic_biology" title="Synthetic biology">synthetic biology</a>. It involves research such as the creation of <a href="/wiki/Synthetic_DNA" class="mw-redirect" title="Synthetic DNA">synthetic DNA</a>. The term "wet" is an extension of the term "<a href="/wiki/Wetware_(brain)" title="Wetware (brain)">wetware</a>". Efforts toward "wet" artificial life focus on engineering live minimal cells from living bacteria <i><a href="/wiki/Mycoplasma_laboratorium" title="Mycoplasma laboratorium">Mycoplasma laboratorium</a></i> and in building non-living biochemical cell-like systems from scratch.
</p><p>In May 2019, researchers reported a new milestone in the creation of a new <a href="/wiki/Synthetic_biology#Synthetic_life" title="Synthetic biology">synthetic</a> (possibly <a class="mw-selflink-fragment" href="#Biochemical-based_(&quot;wet&quot;)">artificial</a>) form of <a href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/viability" class="extiw" title="wikt:viability">viable</a> <a href="/wiki/Life" title="Life">life</a>, a variant of the <a href="/wiki/Bacteria" title="Bacteria">bacteria</a> <i><a href="/wiki/Escherichia_coli" title="Escherichia coli">Escherichia coli</a></i>, by reducing the natural number of 64 <a href="/wiki/Codon" class="mw-redirect" title="Codon">codons</a> in the bacterial <a href="/wiki/Genome" title="Genome">genome</a> to 59 codons instead, in order to encode 20 <a href="/wiki/Amino_acid" title="Amino acid">amino acids</a>.<sup id="cite&#95;ref-NYT-20190515&#95;18-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-NYT-20190515-18"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>18<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup><sup id="cite&#95;ref-NAT-20190515&#95;19-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-NAT-20190515-19"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>19<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup>
</p><p>In 2020, Sam Kriegman and Douglas Blackiston reported the creation of a <a href="/wiki/Xenobot" title="Xenobot">biological robot</a> aided by <a href="/wiki/Artificial_intelligence" title="Artificial intelligence">artificial intelligence</a><sup id="cite&#95;ref-20" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-20"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>20<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup>.
</p><p>In 2021, the same team that developed Xenobots reported a further breakthrough: the first biological robots capable of <b>kinematic self-replication</b>. Unlike traditional biological reproduction (growth/birth), these synthetic organisms spontaneously collect loose cells in their environment to assemble new copies of themselves, a process previously seen only at the molecular level.<sup id="cite&#95;ref-21" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-21"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>21<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup>
</p>
<div class="mw-heading mw-heading2"><h2 id="Open_problems">Open problems</h2><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Artificial_life&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Open problems"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></div>
<dl><dt><a href="/wiki/Abiogenesis" title="Abiogenesis">How does life arise from the nonliving?</a><sup id="cite&#95;ref-22" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-22"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>22<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup><sup id="cite&#95;ref-23" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-23"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>23<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup></dt></dl>
<ul><li>Generate a molecular proto-organism <a href="/wiki/In_vitro" title="In vitro">in vitro</a>.</li>
<li>Achieve the transition to life in an <a href="/wiki/Artificial_chemistry" title="Artificial chemistry">artificial chemistry</a> <a href="/wiki/In_silico" title="In silico">in silico</a>.</li>
<li>Determine whether fundamentally novel living organizations can exist.</li>
<li>Simulate a <a href="/wiki/Unicellular_organism" title="Unicellular organism">unicellular organism</a> over its entire life cycle.</li>
<li>Explain how rules and symbols are generated from physical dynamics in living systems.</li></ul>
<dl><dt>What are the potentials and limits of living systems?</dt></dl>
<ul><li>Determine what is inevitable in the open-ended <a href="/wiki/Evolution_of_life" class="mw-redirect" title="Evolution of life">evolution of life</a>.</li>
<li>Determine minimal conditions for evolutionary transitions from specific to generic response systems.</li>
<li>Create a formal framework for synthesizing dynamical hierarchies at all scales.</li>
<li>Determine the predictability of evolutionary consequences of manipulating organisms and ecosystems.</li>
<li>Develop a theory of <a href="/wiki/Information_processing_(psychology)" title="Information processing (psychology)">information processing</a>, <a href="/wiki/Information_flow_(information_theory)" title="Information flow (information theory)">information flow</a>, and information generation for evolving systems.</li></ul>
<dl><dt>How is life related to mind, machines, and culture?</dt></dl>
<ul><li>Demonstrate the emergence of intelligence and mind in an artificial living system.</li>
<li>Evaluate the influence of machines on the next major evolutionary transition of life.</li>
<li>Provide a quantitative model of the interplay between cultural and biological evolution.</li>
<li>Establish <a href="/wiki/Ethical" class="mw-redirect" title="Ethical">ethical</a> principles for artificial life.</li></ul>

<div class="mw-heading mw-heading2"><h2 id="Criticism">Criticism</h2><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Artificial_life&amp;action=edit&amp;section=16" title="Edit section: Criticism"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></div>
<p>Artificial life has had a controversial history. <a href="/wiki/John_Maynard_Smith" title="John Maynard Smith">John Maynard Smith</a> criticized certain artificial life work in 1994 as "fact-free science".<sup id="cite&#95;ref-25" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-25"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>25<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Mario_Bunge" title="Mario Bunge">Mario Bunge</a> criticized the ideas of strong artificial life as part of his wider critique of <a href="/wiki/Computationalism" class="mw-redirect" title="Computationalism">computationalism</a>. He wrote that proponents of strong alife are mistakenly erasing the distinction between a simulation and the process that is being simulated. He had no such objections to the weak alife program.<sup id="cite&#95;ref-26" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-26"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>26<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup>
