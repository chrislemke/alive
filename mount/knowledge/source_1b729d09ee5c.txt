<p>In the <a href="/wiki/Mathematics" title="Mathematics">mathematical</a> field of <a href="/wiki/Dynamical_system" title="Dynamical system">dynamical systems</a>, an <b>attractor</b> is a set of states toward which a system tends to evolve,<sup id="cite&#95;ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>2<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> for a wide variety of starting conditions of the system. System values that get close enough to the attractor values remain close even if slightly disturbed.
</p><p>In finite-dimensional systems, the evolving variable may be represented <a href="/wiki/Algebra" title="Algebra">algebraically</a> as an <i>n</i>-dimensional <a href="/wiki/Coordinate_vector" title="Coordinate vector">vector</a>. The attractor is a region in <a href="/wiki/Space_(mathematics)" title="Space (mathematics)"><i>n</i>-dimensional space</a>. In <a href="/wiki/Physics" title="Physics">physical systems</a>, the <i>n</i> dimensions may be, for example, two or three positional coordinates for each of one or more physical entities; in <a href="/wiki/Economics" title="Economics">economic systems</a>, they may be separate variables such as the <a href="/wiki/Inflation_rate" class="mw-redirect" title="Inflation rate">inflation rate</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Unemployment_rate" class="mw-redirect" title="Unemployment rate">unemployment rate</a>.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources in the body of the article. (March 2022)">not verified in body</span></a></i>&#93;</sup>
</p><p>If the evolving variable is two- or three-dimensional, the attractor of the dynamic process can be represented <a href="/wiki/Geometry" title="Geometry">geometrically</a> in two or three dimensions, (as for example in the three-dimensional case depicted to the right). An attractor can be a <a href="/wiki/Point_(geometry)" title="Point (geometry)">point</a>, a finite set of points, a <a href="/wiki/Curve" title="Curve">curve</a>, a <a href="/wiki/Manifold" title="Manifold">manifold</a>, or even a complicated set with a <a href="/wiki/Fractal" title="Fractal">fractal</a> structure known as a <i>strange attractor</i> (see <a href="#Strange_attractor">strange attractor</a> below). If the variable is a <a href="/wiki/Scalar_(mathematics)" title="Scalar (mathematics)">scalar</a>, the attractor is a subset of the real number line. Describing the attractors of chaotic dynamical systems has been one of the achievements of <a href="/wiki/Chaos_theory" title="Chaos theory">chaos theory</a>.
</p><p>A <a href="/wiki/Trajectory" title="Trajectory">trajectory</a> of the dynamical system in the attractor does not have to satisfy any special constraints except for remaining on the attractor, forward in time.  The trajectory may be <a href="/wiki/Periodic_function" title="Periodic function">periodic</a> or <a href="/wiki/Chaos_theory" title="Chaos theory">chaotic</a>. If a set of points is periodic or chaotic, but the flow in the neighborhood is away from the set, the set is not an attractor, but instead is called a <i>repeller</i> (or <i>repellor</i>).
</p>

<div class="mw-heading mw-heading2"><h2 id="Motivation_of_attractors">Motivation of attractors</h2><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Attractor&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Motivation of attractors"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></div>
<p>A <a href="/wiki/Dynamical_system" title="Dynamical system">dynamical system</a> is generally described by one or more <a href="/wiki/Differential_equations" class="mw-redirect" title="Differential equations">differential</a> or <a href="/wiki/Difference_equations" class="mw-redirect" title="Difference equations">difference equations</a>. The equations of a given dynamical system specify its behavior over any given short period of time. To determine the system's behavior for a longer period, it is often necessary to <a href="/wiki/Integral" title="Integral">integrate</a> the equations, either through analytical means or through <a href="/wiki/Iteration" title="Iteration">iteration</a>, often with the aid of computers.
</p><p>Dynamical systems in the physical world tend to arise from <a href="/wiki/Dissipative_system" title="Dissipative system">dissipative systems</a>: if it were not for some driving force, the motion would cease.  (Dissipation may come from <a href="/wiki/Friction" title="Friction">internal friction</a>, <a href="/wiki/Thermodynamics" title="Thermodynamics">thermodynamic losses</a>, or loss of material, among many causes.) The dissipation and the driving force tend to balance, killing off initial transients and settle the system into its typical behavior. The subset of the <a href="/wiki/Phase_space" title="Phase space">phase space</a> of the dynamical system corresponding to the typical behavior is the attractor, also known as the attracting section or attractee.
</p><p>Invariant sets and <a href="/wiki/Limit_set" title="Limit set">limit sets</a> are similar to the attractor concept. An <i>invariant set</i> is a set that evolves to itself under the dynamics.<sup id="cite&#95;ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>3<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> Attractors may contain invariant sets. A <i>limit set</i> is a set of points such that there exists some initial state that ends up arbitrarily close to the limit set (i.e. to each point of the set) as time goes to infinity. Attractors are limit sets, but not all limit sets are attractors: It is possible to have some points of a system converge to a limit set, but different points when perturbed slightly off the limit set may get knocked off and never return to the vicinity of the limit set.
</p><p>For example, the <a href="/wiki/Damping_ratio" class="mw-redirect" title="Damping ratio">damped</a> <a href="/wiki/Pendulum" title="Pendulum">pendulum</a> has two invariant points: the point <span class="texhtml"><i>x</i><sub>0</sub></span> of minimum height and the point <span class="texhtml"><i>x</i><sub>1</sub></span> of maximum height. The point <span class="texhtml">x<sub>0</sub></span> is also a limit set, as trajectories converge to it; the point <span class="texhtml"><i>x</i><sub>1</sub></span> is not a limit set.  Because of the dissipation due to air resistance, the point <span class="texhtml">x<sub>0</sub></span> is also an attractor.  If there was no dissipation, <span class="texhtml"><i>x</i><sub>0</sub></span> would not be an attractor. Aristotle believed that objects moved only as long as they were pushed, which is an early formulation of a dissipative attractor.
</p><p>Some attractors are known to be chaotic (see <a href="#Strange_attractor">strange attractor</a>), in which case the evolution of any two distinct points of the attractor result in exponentially <a href="/wiki/Chaos_theory" title="Chaos theory">diverging trajectories</a>, which complicates prediction when even the smallest noise is present in the system.<sup id="cite&#95;ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>4<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup>
</p>

<div class="mw-heading mw-heading2"><h2 id="Types_of_attractors">Types of attractors</h2><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Attractor&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Types of attractors"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></div>
<p>Attractors are portions or <a href="/wiki/Subset" title="Subset">subsets</a> of the <a href="/wiki/Configuration_space_(physics)" title="Configuration space (physics)">phase space</a> of a <a href="/wiki/Dynamical_system" title="Dynamical system">dynamical system</a>. Until the 1960s, attractors were thought of as being <a href="/wiki/Geometric_primitive" title="Geometric primitive">simple geometric subsets</a> of the phase space, like <a href="/wiki/Point_(geometry)" title="Point (geometry)">points</a>, <a href="/wiki/Line_(mathematics)" class="mw-redirect" title="Line (mathematics)">lines</a>, <a href="/wiki/Surface_(topology)" title="Surface (topology)">surfaces</a>, and simple regions of <a href="/wiki/Three-dimensional_space" title="Three-dimensional space">three-dimensional space</a>. More complex attractors that cannot be categorized as simple geometric subsets, such as <a href="/wiki/Topology" title="Topology">topologically</a> wild sets, were known of at the time but were thought to be fragile anomalies. <a href="/wiki/Stephen_Smale" title="Stephen Smale">Stephen Smale</a> was able to show that his <a href="/wiki/Horseshoe_map" title="Horseshoe map">horseshoe map</a> was <a href="/wiki/Structural_stability" title="Structural stability">robust</a> and that its attractor had the structure of a <a href="/wiki/Cantor_set" title="Cantor set">Cantor set</a>.
</p><p>Two simple attractors are a <a href="/wiki/Fixed_point_(mathematics)" title="Fixed point (mathematics)">fixed point</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Limit_cycle" title="Limit cycle">limit cycle</a>. Attractors can take on many other geometric shapes (phase space subsets). But when these sets (or the motions within them) cannot be easily described as simple combinations (e.g. <a href="/wiki/Intersection_(set_theory)" title="Intersection (set theory)">intersection</a> and <a href="/wiki/Union_(set_theory)" title="Union (set theory)">union</a>) of <a href="/wiki/Geometric_primitive" title="Geometric primitive">fundamental geometric objects</a> (e.g. <a href="/wiki/Line_(mathematics)" class="mw-redirect" title="Line (mathematics)">lines</a>, <a href="/wiki/Surface_(topology)" title="Surface (topology)">surfaces</a>, <a href="/wiki/Sphere" title="Sphere">spheres</a>, <a href="/wiki/Toroid" title="Toroid">toroids</a>, <a href="/wiki/Manifold" title="Manifold">manifolds</a>), then the attractor is called a <i><a href="#Strange_attractor">strange attractor</a></i>.
</p>
<div class="mw-heading mw-heading3"><h3 id="Fixed_point">Fixed point</h3><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Attractor&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Fixed point"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></div>
<figure class="mw-default-size mw-halign-right" typeof="mw:File/Thumb"><a href="/wiki/File:Critical_orbit_3d.png" class="mw-file-description"><img src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/07/Critical_orbit_3d.png/250px-Critical_orbit_3d.png" decoding="async" width="250" height="125" class="mw-file-element" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/07/Critical_orbit_3d.png/500px-Critical_orbit_3d.png 1.5x" data-file-width="2000" data-file-height="1000" /></a><figcaption>Weakly attracting fixed point for a complex number evolving according to a <a href="/wiki/Complex_quadratic_polynomial" title="Complex quadratic polynomial">complex quadratic polynomial</a>. The phase space is the horizontal complex plane; the vertical axis measures the frequency with which points in the complex plane are visited. The point in the complex plane directly below the peak frequency is the fixed point attractor.</figcaption></figure>
<p>A <a href="/wiki/Fixed_point_(mathematics)" title="Fixed point (mathematics)">fixed point</a> of a function or transformation is a point that is mapped to itself by the function or transformation. If we regard the evolution of a dynamical system as a series of transformations, then there may or may not be a point which remains fixed under each transformation. The final state that a dynamical system evolves towards corresponds to an attracting fixed point of the evolution function for that system, such as the center bottom position of a <a href="/wiki/Damping_ratio" class="mw-redirect" title="Damping ratio">damped</a> <a href="/wiki/Pendulum" title="Pendulum">pendulum</a>, the level and flat water line of sloshing water in a glass, or the bottom center of a bowl containing a rolling marble. But the fixed point(s) of a dynamic system is not necessarily an attractor of the system. For example, if the bowl containing a rolling marble was inverted and the marble was balanced on top of the bowl, the center bottom (now top) of the bowl is a fixed state, but not an attractor. This is equivalent to the difference between <a href="/wiki/Stability_theory#Stability_of_fixed_points" title="Stability theory">stable and unstable equilibria</a>. In the case of a marble on top of an inverted bowl (a hill), that point at the top of the bowl (hill) is a fixed point (equilibrium), but not an attractor (unstable equilibrium).
</p><p>In addition, physical dynamic systems with at least one fixed point invariably have multiple fixed points and attractors due to the reality of dynamics in the physical world, including the <a href="/wiki/Nonlinear_dynamics" class="mw-redirect" title="Nonlinear dynamics">nonlinear dynamics</a> of <a href="/wiki/Stiction" title="Stiction">stiction</a>, <a href="/wiki/Friction" title="Friction">friction</a>, <a href="/wiki/Surface_roughness" title="Surface roughness">surface roughness</a>, <a href="/wiki/Deformation_(engineering)" title="Deformation (engineering)">deformation</a> (both <a href="/wiki/Elastic_deformation" class="mw-redirect" title="Elastic deformation">elastic</a> and <a href="/wiki/Plastic" title="Plastic">plasticity</a>), and even <a href="/wiki/Quantum_mechanics" title="Quantum mechanics">quantum mechanics</a>.<sup id="cite&#95;ref-Contact&#95;of&#95;Nominally&#95;Flat&#95;Surfaces&#95;6-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Contact_of_Nominally_Flat_Surfaces-6"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>6<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> In the case of a marble on top of an inverted bowl, even if the bowl seems perfectly <a href="/wiki/Sphere#Hemisphere" title="Sphere">hemispherical</a>, and the marble's <a href="/wiki/Sphere" title="Sphere">spherical</a> shape, are both much more complex surfaces when examined under a microscope, and their <a href="/wiki/Contact_mechanics#History" title="Contact mechanics">shapes change</a> or <a href="/wiki/Deformation_(physics)" title="Deformation (physics)">deform</a> during contact. Any physical surface can be seen to have a rough terrain of multiple peaks, valleys, saddle points, ridges, ravines, and plains.<sup id="cite&#95;ref-NISTIR&#95;89-4088&#95;7-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-NISTIR_89-4088-7"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>7<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> There are many points in this surface terrain (and the dynamic system of a similarly rough marble rolling around on this microscopic terrain) that are considered <a href="/wiki/Critical_point_(mathematics)" title="Critical point (mathematics)">stationary</a> or fixed points, some of which are categorized as attractors.
</p>
<div class="mw-heading mw-heading3"><h3 id="Finite_number_of_points">Finite number of points</h3><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Attractor&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Finite number of points"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></div>
<p>In a <a href="/wiki/Discrete_time_and_continuous_time" title="Discrete time and continuous time">discrete-time</a> system, an attractor can take the form of a finite number of points that are visited in sequence. Each of these points is called a <a href="/wiki/Periodic_point" title="Periodic point">periodic point</a>. This is illustrated by the <a href="/wiki/Logistic_map" title="Logistic map">logistic map</a>, which depending on its specific parameter value can have an attractor consisting of 1 point, 2 points, 2<sup><i>n</i></sup> points, 3 points, 3×2<sup><i>n</i></sup> points, 4 points, 5 points, or any given positive integer number of points.
</p>
<div class="mw-heading mw-heading3"><h3 id="Limit_cycle">Limit cycle</h3><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Attractor&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Limit cycle"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></div>
<link rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style" href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r1320445320" /><div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Limit_cycle" title="Limit cycle">Limit cycle</a></div>
<p>A <a href="/wiki/Limit_cycle" title="Limit cycle">limit cycle</a> is a periodic orbit of a continuous dynamical system that is <a href="/wiki/Isolated_point" title="Isolated point">isolated</a>. It concerns a <a href="/wiki/Attractor_network" title="Attractor network">cyclic attractor</a>. Examples include the swings of a <a href="/wiki/Pendulum_clock" title="Pendulum clock">pendulum clock</a>, and the heartbeat while resting. The limit cycle of an ideal pendulum is not an example of a limit cycle attractor because its orbits are not isolated: in the phase space of the ideal pendulum, near any point of a periodic orbit there is another point that belongs to a different periodic orbit, so the former orbit is not attracting. For a physical pendulum under friction, the resting state will be a fixed-point attractor. The difference with the clock pendulum is that there, energy is injected by the <a href="/wiki/Escapement" title="Escapement">escapement</a> mechanism to maintain the cycle.
</p>

<div class="mw-heading mw-heading3"><h3 id="Limit_torus">Limit torus</h3><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Attractor&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Limit torus"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></div>
<p>There may be more than one frequency in the periodic trajectory of the system through the state of a limit cycle. For example, in physics, one frequency may dictate the rate at which a planet orbits a star while a second frequency describes the oscillations in the distance between the two bodies. If two of these frequencies form an <a href="/wiki/Irrational_number" title="Irrational number">irrational fraction</a> (i.e. they are <a href="/wiki/Commensurability_(mathematics)" title="Commensurability (mathematics)">incommensurate</a>), the trajectory is no longer closed, and the limit cycle becomes a limit <a href="/wiki/Torus" title="Torus">torus</a>. This kind of attractor is called an <span class="texhtml"><i>N</i><sub><i>t</i></sub></span> -torus if there are <span class="texhtml">N<sub>t</sub></span> incommensurate frequencies. For example, here is a 2-torus:

<div class="mw-heading mw-heading3"><h3 id="Strange_attractor">Strange attractor</h3><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Attractor&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Strange attractor"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></div>
<link rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style" href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r1320445320" /><div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">"Strange attractor" redirects here. For other uses, see <a href="/wiki/Strange_Attractor_(disambiguation)" class="mw-redirect mw-disambig" title="Strange Attractor (disambiguation)">Strange Attractor (disambiguation)</a>.</div>
<figure class="mw-halign-right" typeof="mw:File/Thumb"><a href="/wiki/File:Lorenz_attractor_yb.svg" class="mw-file-description"><img src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/5b/Lorenz_attractor_yb.svg/250px-Lorenz_attractor_yb.svg.png" decoding="async" width="200" height="200" class="mw-file-element" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/5b/Lorenz_attractor_yb.svg/330px-Lorenz_attractor_yb.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/5b/Lorenz_attractor_yb.svg/500px-Lorenz_attractor_yb.svg.png 2x" data-file-width="750" data-file-height="750" /></a><figcaption>A plot of <a href="/wiki/Lorenz%27s_strange_attractor" class="mw-redirect" title="Lorenz&#39;s strange attractor">Lorenz's strange attractor</a> for values&#160;<i>ρ</i>&#160;=&#160;28,&#160;<i>σ</i>&#160;=&#160;10,&#160;<i>β</i>&#160;=&#160;8/3</figcaption></figure>
<p>An attractor is called <i>strange</i> if it has a <a href="/wiki/Fractal" title="Fractal">fractal</a> structure, that is if it has non-integer <a href="/wiki/Hausdorff_dimension" title="Hausdorff dimension">Hausdorff dimension</a>. This is often the case when the dynamics on it are <a href="/wiki/Chaos_theory" title="Chaos theory">chaotic</a>, but <a href="/wiki/Strange_nonchaotic_attractor" title="Strange nonchaotic attractor">strange nonchaotic attractors</a> also exist. If a strange attractor is chaotic, exhibiting <a href="/wiki/Sensitive_dependence_on_initial_conditions" class="mw-redirect" title="Sensitive dependence on initial conditions">sensitive dependence on initial conditions</a>, then any two arbitrarily close alternative initial points on the attractor, after any of various numbers of iterations, will lead to points that are arbitrarily far apart (subject to the confines of the attractor), and after any of various other numbers of iterations will lead to points that are arbitrarily close together. Thus a dynamic system with a chaotic attractor is locally unstable yet globally stable: once some sequences have entered the attractor, nearby points diverge from one another but never depart from the attractor.<sup id="cite&#95;ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>8<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup>
</p><p>The term <i>strange attractor</i> was coined by <a href="/wiki/David_Ruelle" title="David Ruelle">David Ruelle</a> and <a href="/wiki/Floris_Takens" title="Floris Takens">Floris Takens</a> to describe the attractor resulting from a series of <a href="/wiki/Bifurcation_theory" title="Bifurcation theory">bifurcations</a> of a system describing fluid flow.<sup id="cite&#95;ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>9<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> Strange attractors are often <a href="/wiki/Differentiable_function" title="Differentiable function">differentiable</a> in a few directions, but some are <a href="/wiki/Homeomorphic" class="mw-redirect" title="Homeomorphic">like</a> a <a href="/wiki/Cantor_dust" class="mw-redirect" title="Cantor dust">Cantor dust</a>, and therefore not differentiable. Strange attractors may also be found in the presence of noise, where they may be shown to support invariant random probability measures of Sinai–Ruelle–Bowen type.<sup id="cite&#95;ref-Stochastic&#95;climate&#95;dynamics:&#95;Random&#95;attractors&#95;and&#95;time-dependent&#95;invariant&#95;measures&#95;10-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Stochastic_climate_dynamics:_Random_attractors_and_time-dependent_invariant_measures-10"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>10<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup>
</p><p>Examples of strange attractors include the <a href="/wiki/Double_scroll_attractor" class="mw-redirect" title="Double scroll attractor">double-scroll attractor</a>, <a href="/wiki/H%C3%A9non_map" title="Hénon map">Hénon attractor</a>, <a href="/wiki/R%C3%B6ssler_attractor" title="Rössler attractor">Rössler attractor</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Lorenz_attractor" class="mw-redirect" title="Lorenz attractor">Lorenz attractor</a>.
</p>

It follows from the <a href="/wiki/Sharkovskii%27s_theorem" title="Sharkovskii&#39;s theorem">Sharkovskii's theorem</a> that orbits of any natural period are present in the system. Thus one dynamic equation can have vastly different attractors depending on the choice of parameters.
</p>
<div class="mw-heading mw-heading2"><h2 id="Basins_of_attraction">Basins of attraction</h2><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Attractor&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Basins of attraction"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></div>
<p>An attractor's basin of attraction is the region of the <a href="/wiki/Phase_space" title="Phase space">phase space</a>, over which iterations are defined, such that any point (any <a href="/wiki/Initial_condition" title="Initial condition">initial condition</a>) in that region will <a href="/wiki/Asymptotic_behavior" class="mw-redirect" title="Asymptotic behavior">asymptotically</a> be iterated into the attractor. For a <a href="/wiki/Stability_(mathematics)" class="mw-redirect" title="Stability (mathematics)">stable</a> <a href="/wiki/Linear_system" title="Linear system">linear system</a>, every point in the phase space is in the basin of attraction. However, in <a href="/wiki/Nonlinear_system" title="Nonlinear system">nonlinear systems</a>, some points may map directly or asymptotically to infinity, while other points may lie in a different basin of attraction and map asymptotically into a different attractor; other initial conditions may be in or map directly into a non-attracting point or cycle.<sup id="cite&#95;ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>11<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup>
</p>

<div class="mw-heading mw-heading2"><h2 id="Partial_differential_equations">Partial differential equations</h2><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Attractor&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Partial differential equations"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></div>
<p><a href="/wiki/Parabolic_partial_differential_equation" title="Parabolic partial differential equation">Parabolic partial differential equations</a> may have finite-dimensional attractors. The diffusive part of the equation damps higher frequencies and in some cases leads to a global attractor. The <i>Ginzburg–Landau</i>, the <i>Kuramoto–Sivashinsky</i>, and the two-dimensional, forced <a href="/wiki/Navier%E2%80%93Stokes_equation" class="mw-redirect" title="Navier–Stokes equation">Navier–Stokes equations</a> are all known to have global attractors of finite dimension.
</p><p>For the three-dimensional, incompressible Navier–Stokes equation with periodic <a href="/wiki/Boundary_condition" class="mw-redirect" title="Boundary condition">boundary conditions</a>, if it has a global attractor, then this attractor will be of finite dimensions.<sup id="cite&#95;ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>13<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup>
