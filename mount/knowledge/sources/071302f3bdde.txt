</div><div class="mw-content-ltr mw-parser-output" lang="en" dir="ltr"><div class="shortdescription nomobile noexcerpt noprint searchaux" style="display:none">System capable of producing itself</div>
<figure class="mw-default-size" typeof="mw:File/Thumb"><a href="/wiki/File:3D-SIM-4_Anaphase_3_color.jpg" class="mw-file-description"><img src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e0/3D-SIM-4_Anaphase_3_color.jpg/250px-3D-SIM-4_Anaphase_3_color.jpg" decoding="async" width="250" height="190" class="mw-file-element" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e0/3D-SIM-4_Anaphase_3_color.jpg/500px-3D-SIM-4_Anaphase_3_color.jpg 1.5x" data-file-width="954" data-file-height="724" /></a><figcaption>3D representation of a living cell during the process of <a href="/wiki/Mitosis" title="Mitosis">mitosis</a>, example of an autopoietic system</figcaption></figure>
<p>The term <b>autopoiesis</b> (from&#32;<a href="/wiki/Greek_language" title="Greek language">Greek</a>&#32;<i> </i>αὐτo-<i> (auto)</i>&#160;<span class="gloss-quot">'</span><span class="gloss-text">self</span><span class="gloss-quot">'</span>&#32;and&#32;<i> </i>ποίησις<i> (<a href="/wiki/Poiesis" title="Poiesis">poiesis</a>)</i>&#160;<span class="gloss-quot">'</span><span class="gloss-text">creation, production</span><span class="gloss-quot">'</span>), one of several current theories of life, refers to a <a href="/wiki/System" title="System">system</a> capable of producing and maintaining itself by creating its own parts.<sup id="cite&#95;ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>1<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup>
The term was introduced in the 1972 publication <i><a href="/wiki/Autopoiesis_and_Cognition:_The_Realization_of_the_Living" title="Autopoiesis and Cognition: The Realization of the Living">Autopoiesis and Cognition: The Realization of the Living</a></i>  by Chilean biologists <a href="/wiki/Humberto_Maturana" title="Humberto Maturana">Humberto Maturana</a> and <a href="/wiki/Francisco_Varela" title="Francisco Varela">Francisco Varela</a> to define the self-maintaining <a href="/wiki/Chemistry" title="Chemistry">chemistry</a> of living <a href="/wiki/Cells_(biology)" class="mw-redirect" title="Cells (biology)">cells</a>.<sup id="cite&#95;ref-Maturana&#95;Varela&#95;1972&#95;2-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Maturana_Varela_1972-2"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>2<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup>
</p><p>The concept has since been applied to the fields of <a href="/wiki/Cognition" title="Cognition">cognition</a>, <a href="/wiki/Neuroscience" title="Neuroscience">neurobiology</a>, <a href="/wiki/Systems_theory" title="Systems theory">systems theory</a>, <a href="/wiki/Architecture" title="Architecture">architecture</a> and <a href="/wiki/Sociology" title="Sociology">sociology</a>. <a href="/wiki/Niklas_Luhmann" title="Niklas Luhmann">Niklas Luhmann</a> briefly introduced the concept of autopoiesis to <a href="/wiki/Organizational_theory" title="Organizational theory">organizational theory</a>.<sup id="cite&#95;ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>3<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup>
</p>
<meta property="mw:PageProp/toc" />
<div class="mw-heading mw-heading2"><h2 id="Overview">Overview</h2><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Autopoiesis&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Overview"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></div>
<p>Maturana describes how he invented the word <i>autopoiesis</i>:<sup id="cite&#95;ref-Maturana&#95;Varela&#95;1980&#95;4-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Maturana_Varela_1980-4"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>4<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup><sup class="reference nowrap"><span title="Page / location: xvii">&#58;&#8202;xvii&#8202;</span></sup> <sup id="cite&#95;ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>5<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup>
</p>
<style data-mw-deduplicate="TemplateStyles:r1244412712">.mw-parser-output .templatequote{overflow:hidden;margin:1em 0;padding:0 32px}.mw-parser-output .templatequotecite{line-height:1.5em;text-align:left;margin-top:0}@media(min-width:500px){.mw-parser-output .templatequotecite{padding-left:1.6em}}</style><blockquote class="templatequote"><p>"... one day, while talking with a friend (José Bulnes) about an essay of his on Don Quixote de la Mancha, in which he analyzed Don Quixote's dilemma of whether to follow the path of arms (<i>praxis</i>, action) or the path of letters (<i>poiesis</i>, creation, production), and his eventual choice of the path of <i>praxis</i> deferring any attempt at <i>poiesis</i>, I understood for the first time the power of the word 'poiesis' and invented the word that we needed: <i>autopoiesis</i>. This was a word without a history, a word that could directly mean what takes place in the dynamics of the autonomy proper to living systems." </p></blockquote><div class="templatequotecite"><p style="display: inline; padding-left: 2.3em;">—&#8202;Humberto Maturana, <i><a href="/wiki/Autopoiesis_and_Cognition:_The_Realization_of_the_Living" title="Autopoiesis and Cognition: The Realization of the Living">Autopoiesis and Cognition</a></i>, <i>Introduction</i>, <a rel="nofollow" class="external text" href="https://www.google.com/books/edition/Autopoiesis_and_Cognition/nVmcN9Ja68kC?hl=en&amp;gbpv=1&amp;dq=don%20quixote&amp;pg=PR17&amp;printsec=frontcover">page xvii</a><span style="display:none" data-plural="0"></span></p></div>
<p>Maturana and Varela explain that:<sup id="cite&#95;ref-Maturana&#95;Varela&#95;1980&#95;4-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Maturana_Varela_1980-4"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>4<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup><sup class="reference nowrap"><span title="Page / location: 78">&#58;&#8202;78&#8202;</span></sup>
</p>
<link rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style" href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r1244412712" /><blockquote class="templatequote"><p>"An autopoietic machine is a machine organized (defined as a unity) as a network of processes of production (transformation and destruction) of components which: (i) through their interactions and transformations continuously regenerate and realize the network of processes (relations) that produced them; and (ii) constitute it (the machine) as a concrete unity in space in which they (the components) exist by specifying the topological domain of its realization as such a network."</p></blockquote>
<p>They describe the "space defined by an autopoietic system" as "self-contained", a space that "cannot be described by using dimensions that define another space. When we refer to our interactions with a concrete autopoietic system, however, we project this system on the space of our manipulations and make a description of this projection."<sup id="cite&#95;ref-Maturana&#95;Varela&#95;1980&#95;4-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Maturana_Varela_1980-4"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>4<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup><sup class="reference nowrap"><span title="Page / location: 89">&#58;&#8202;89&#8202;</span></sup>
</p>
<div class="mw-heading mw-heading2"><h2 id="Meaning">Meaning</h2><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Autopoiesis&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Meaning"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></div>
<p>Autopoiesis was originally presented as a system description that was said to define and explain the nature of <a href="/wiki/Living_systems" title="Living systems">living systems</a>. A canonical example of an autopoietic system is the <a href="/wiki/Biological_cell" class="mw-redirect" title="Biological cell">biological cell</a>. The <a href="/wiki/Eukaryote" title="Eukaryote">eukaryotic</a> cell, for example, is made of various <a href="/wiki/Biochemical" class="mw-redirect" title="Biochemical">biochemical</a> components such as <a href="/wiki/Nucleic_acid" title="Nucleic acid">nucleic acids</a> and <a href="/wiki/Protein" title="Protein">proteins</a>, and is organized into bounded structures such as the <a href="/wiki/Cell_nucleus" title="Cell nucleus">cell nucleus</a>, various <a href="/wiki/Organelle" title="Organelle">organelles</a>, a <a href="/wiki/Cell_membrane" title="Cell membrane">cell membrane</a> and <a href="/wiki/Cytoskeleton" title="Cytoskeleton">cytoskeleton</a>. These structures, based on an internal flow of molecules and energy, <i>produce</i> the components which, in turn, continue to maintain the organized bounded structure that gives rise to these components.
</p><p>An autopoietic system is to be contrasted with an <a href="/wiki/Allopoietic" class="mw-redirect" title="Allopoietic">allopoietic</a> system, such as a car factory, which uses raw materials (components) to generate a car (an organized structure) which is something <i>other</i> than itself (the factory). However, if the system is extended from the factory to include components in the factory's "environment", such as supply chains, plant / equipment, workers, dealerships, customers, contracts, competitors, cars, spare parts, and so on, then as a total viable system it could be considered to be autopoietic.<sup id="cite&#95;ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>6<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup>
</p><p>Autopoiesis in biological systems can be viewed as a network of constraints that work to maintain themselves. This concept has been called organizational closure<sup id="cite&#95;ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>7<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> or constraint closure<sup id="cite&#95;ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>8<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> and is closely related to the study of <a href="/wiki/Autocatalysis" title="Autocatalysis">autocatalytic chemical networks</a> where constraints are reactions required to sustain life.
</p><p>Though others have often used the term as a synonym for <a href="/wiki/Self-organization" title="Self-organization">self-organization</a>, Maturana himself stated he would "[n]ever use the notion of self-organization&#160;... Operationally it is impossible. That is, if the organization of a thing changes, the thing changes".<sup id="cite&#95;ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>9<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> Moreover, an autopoietic system is autonomous and operationally closed, in the sense that there are sufficient processes within it to maintain the whole. Autopoietic systems are "structurally coupled" with their medium, embedded in a dynamic of changes that can be recalled as <a href="/wiki/Sensory-motor_coupling" title="Sensory-motor coupling">sensory-motor coupling</a>.<sup id="cite&#95;ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>10<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> This continuous dynamic is considered as a rudimentary form of <a href="/wiki/Knowledge" title="Knowledge">knowledge</a> or <a href="/wiki/Cognition" title="Cognition">cognition</a> and can be observed throughout life-forms.
</p><p>An application of the concept of autopoiesis to <a href="/wiki/Sociology" title="Sociology">sociology</a> can be found in Niklas Luhmann's <a href="/wiki/Systems_theory#Sociology_and_Sociocybernetics" title="Systems theory">Systems Theory</a>, which was subsequently adapted by <a href="/wiki/Bob_Jessop" title="Bob Jessop">Bob Jessop</a> in his studies of the capitalist state system. <a href="/w/index.php?title=Marjatta_Maula&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Marjatta Maula (page does not exist)">Marjatta Maula</a> adapted the concept of autopoiesis in a business context.<sup id="cite&#95;ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>11<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> The theory of autopoiesis has also been applied in the context of legal systems by not only Niklas Luhmann, but also Gunther Teubner.<sup id="cite&#95;ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>12<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup><sup id="cite&#95;ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>13<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Patrik_Schumacher" title="Patrik Schumacher">Patrik Schumacher</a> has applied the term to refer to the 'discursive self-referential making of architecture.' <sup id="cite&#95;ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>14<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup><sup id="cite&#95;ref-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-15"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>15<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> Varela eventually further applied autopoesis to develop models of mind, brain, and behavior called non-<a href="/wiki/Representationalist" class="mw-redirect" title="Representationalist">representationalist</a>, <a href="/wiki/Enactivism" title="Enactivism">enactive</a>, <a href="/wiki/Embodied_cognitive_science" title="Embodied cognitive science">embodied cognitive neuroscience</a>, culminating in <a href="/wiki/Neurophenomenology" title="Neurophenomenology">neurophenomenology</a>.
</p><p>In the context of textual studies, <a href="/wiki/Jerome_McGann" title="Jerome McGann">Jerome McGann</a> argues that texts are "autopoietic mechanisms operating as self-generating feedback systems that cannot be separated from those who manipulate and use them".<sup id="cite&#95;ref-16" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-16"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>16<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> Citing Maturana and Varela, he defines an autopoietic system as "a closed topological space that 'continuously generates and specifies its own organization through its operation as a system of production of its own components, and does this in an endless turnover of components<span style="padding-right:.15em;">'</span>", concluding that "Autopoietic systems are thus distinguished from allopoietic systems, which are Cartesian and which 'have as the product of their functioning something different from themselves<span style="padding-right:.15em;">'</span>". Coding and markup appear <a href="/wiki/Allopoietic" class="mw-redirect" title="Allopoietic">allopoietic</a>", McGann argues, but are generative parts of the system they serve to maintain, and thus language and print or electronic technology are autopoietic systems.<sup id="cite&#95;ref-17" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-17"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>17<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup><sup class="reference nowrap"><span title="Page / location: 200–1">&#58;&#8202;200–1&#8202;</span></sup>
</p><p>
The philosopher <a href="/wiki/Slavoj_%C5%BDi%C5%BEek" title="Slavoj Žižek">Slavoj Žižek</a>, in his discussion of <a href="/wiki/Georg_Wilhelm_Friedrich_Hegel" title="Georg Wilhelm Friedrich Hegel">Hegel</a>, argues: <link rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style" href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r1244412712" /></p><blockquote class="templatequote"><p>"Hegel is – to use today's terms – the ultimate thinker of autopoiesis, of the process of the emergence of necessary features out of chaotic contingency, the thinker of contingency's gradual self-organisation, of the gradual rise of order out of chaos."<sup id="cite&#95;ref-18" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-18"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>18<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup></p></blockquote>
<div class="mw-heading mw-heading2"><h2 id="Relation_to_complexity">Relation to complexity</h2><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Autopoiesis&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Relation to complexity"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></div>
<p>Autopoiesis can be defined as the ratio between the complexity of a system and the complexity of its environment.<sup id="cite&#95;ref-Fernández&#95;19-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Fernández-19"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>19<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup>
</p>
<link rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style" href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r1244412712" /><blockquote class="templatequote"><p>This generalized view of autopoiesis considers systems as self-producing not in terms of their physical components, but in terms of its organization, which can be measured in terms of information and complexity. In other words, we can describe autopoietic systems as those producing more of their own complexity than the one produced by their environment.</p></blockquote><div class="templatequotecite"><p style="display: inline; padding-left: 2.3em;">—&#8202;Carlos Gershenson, "Requisite Variety, Autopoiesis, and Self-organization"
<sup id="cite&#95;ref-Gershenson&#95;20-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Gershenson-20"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>20<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup><span style="display:none" data-plural="1"></span></p></div>
<p>Autopoiesis has been proposed as a potential mechanism of <a href="/wiki/Abiogenesis" title="Abiogenesis">abiogenesis</a>, by which molecules evolved into more complex cells that could support the development of life.<sup id="cite&#95;ref-frontiers&#95;21-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-frontiers-21"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>21<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup>
</p>
<div class="mw-heading mw-heading3"><h3 id="Comparison_with_other_theories_of_life">Comparison with other theories of life</h3><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Autopoiesis&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Comparison with other theories of life"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></div>
<p>Autopoiesis is just one of several current theories of life, including the <a href="/wiki/Chemoton" title="Chemoton">chemoton</a><sup id="cite&#95;ref-gantibook&#95;22-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-gantibook-22"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>22<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> of <a href="/wiki/Tibor_G%C3%A1nti" title="Tibor Gánti">Tibor Gánti</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Hypercycle_(chemistry)" title="Hypercycle (chemistry)">hypercycle</a> of <a href="/wiki/Manfred_Eigen" title="Manfred Eigen">Manfred Eigen</a> and <a href="/wiki/Peter_Schuster_(theoretical_chemist)" title="Peter Schuster (theoretical chemist)">Peter Schuster</a>,<sup id="cite&#95;ref-23" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-23"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>23<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup>
<sup id="cite&#95;ref-24" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-24"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>24<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup>
<sup id="cite&#95;ref-25" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-25"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>25<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> the <a href="/wiki/Robert_Rosen_(theoretical_biologist)#Complexity_and_complex_scientific_models:_(M,R)_systems" class="mw-redirect" title="Robert Rosen (theoretical biologist)"> (<i>M,R</i>) systems</a><sup id="cite&#95;ref-26" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-26"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>26<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup><sup id="cite&#95;ref-27" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-27"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>27<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> of <a href="/wiki/Robert_Rosen_(theoretical_biologist)" class="mw-redirect" title="Robert Rosen (theoretical biologist)">Robert Rosen</a>, and the <a href="/wiki/Autocatalytic_set" title="Autocatalytic set">autocatalytic sets</a><sup id="cite&#95;ref-28" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-28"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>28<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> of <a href="/wiki/Stuart_Kauffman" title="Stuart Kauffman">Stuart Kauffman</a>, similar to an earlier proposal by <a href="/wiki/Freeman_Dyson" title="Freeman Dyson">Freeman Dyson</a>.<sup id="cite&#95;ref-29" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-29"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>29<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> 
All of these (including autopoiesis) found their original inspiration in Erwin Schrödinger's book <i><a href="/wiki/What_Is_Life%3F" title="What Is Life?">What is Life?</a></i><sup id="cite&#95;ref-30" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-30"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>30<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> but at first they appear to have little in common with one another, largely because the authors did not communicate with one another, and none of them made any reference in their principal publications to any of the other theories.  Nonetheless, there are more similarities than may be obvious at first sight, for example between Gánti and Rosen.<sup id="cite&#95;ref-31" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-31"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>31<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> Until recently<sup id="cite&#95;ref-32" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-32"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>32<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup><sup id="cite&#95;ref-33" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-33"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>33<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup><sup id="cite&#95;ref-34" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-34"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>34<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> there have been almost no attempts to compare the different theories and discuss them together.
</p>
<div class="mw-heading mw-heading2"><h2 id="Relation_to_cognition">Relation to cognition</h2><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Autopoiesis&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Relation to cognition"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></div>
<p>An extensive discussion of the connection of autopoiesis to <a href="/wiki/Cognition" title="Cognition">cognition</a> is provided by Evan Thompson in his 2007 publication, <i>Mind in Life</i>.<sup id="cite&#95;ref-Thompson&#95;2007&#95;auto&#95;35-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Thompson_2007_auto-35"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>35<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> The basic notion of autopoiesis as involving constructive interaction with the environment is extended to include cognition. Initially, Maturana defined cognition as behavior of an organism "with relevance to the maintenance of itself".<sup id="cite&#95;ref-Maturana&#95;Varela&#95;1980&#95;chapter&#95;36-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Maturana_Varela_1980_chapter-36"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>36<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup><sup class="reference nowrap"><span title="Page / location: 13">&#58;&#8202;13&#8202;</span></sup> However, computer models that are self-maintaining but non-cognitive have been devised, so some additional restrictions are needed, and the suggestion is that the maintenance process, to be cognitive, involves readjustment of the internal workings of the system in some <a href="/wiki/Metabolism" title="Metabolism">metabolic process</a>. On this basis it is claimed that autopoiesis is a necessary but not a sufficient condition for cognition.<sup id="cite&#95;ref-Bitbol&#95;37-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Bitbol-37"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>37<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> Thompson wrote that this distinction may or may not be fruitful, but what matters is that living systems involve autopoiesis and (if it is necessary to add this point) cognition as well.<sup id="cite&#95;ref-Thompson&#95;2007&#95;38-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Thompson_2007-38"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>38<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup><sup class="reference nowrap"><span title="Page / location: 127">&#58;&#8202;127&#8202;</span></sup> It can be noted that this definition of 'cognition' is restricted, and does not necessarily entail any awareness or <a href="/wiki/Consciousness" title="Consciousness">consciousness</a> by the living system. With the publication of The Embodied Mind in 1991, Varela, Thompson and Rosch applied autopoesis to make non-<a href="/wiki/Representationalist" class="mw-redirect" title="Representationalist">representationalist</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Enactivism" title="Enactivism">enactive models of mind, brain and behavior</a>, which further developed <a href="/wiki/Embodied_cognitive_science" title="Embodied cognitive science">embodied cognitive neuroscience</a>, later culminating in <a href="/wiki/Neurophenomenology" title="Neurophenomenology">neurophenomenology</a>.
</p>
<div class="mw-heading mw-heading2"><h2 id="Relation_to_consciousness">Relation to consciousness</h2><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Autopoiesis&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Relation to consciousness"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></div>
<p>The connection of autopoiesis to cognition, or if necessary, of living systems to cognition, is an objective assessment ascertainable by observation of a living system.
</p><p>One question that arises is about the connection between cognition seen in this manner and consciousness. The separation of cognition and consciousness recognizes that the organism may be unaware of the substratum where decisions are made. What is the connection between these realms? Thompson refers to this issue as the "<a href="/wiki/Explanatory_gap" title="Explanatory gap">explanatory gap</a>", and one aspect of it is the <a href="/wiki/Hard_problem_of_consciousness" title="Hard problem of consciousness">hard problem of consciousness</a>, how and why we have <a href="/wiki/Qualia" title="Qualia">qualia</a>.<sup id="cite&#95;ref-Thompson&#95;2007&#95;cog&#95;39-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Thompson_2007_cog-39"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>39<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup>
</p><p>A second question is whether autopoiesis can provide a bridge between these concepts. Thompson discusses this issue from the standpoint of <a href="/wiki/Enactivism" title="Enactivism">enactivism</a>. An autopoietic cell actively relates to its environment. Its sensory responses trigger motor behavior governed by autopoiesis, and this behavior (it is claimed) is a simplified version of a nervous system behavior. The further claim is that real-time interactions like this require attention, and an implication of attention is awareness.<sup id="cite&#95;ref-Thompson&#95;2007&#95;sensor&#95;40-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Thompson_2007_sensor-40"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>40<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup>
</p>
<div class="mw-heading mw-heading2"><h2 id="Criticism">Criticism</h2><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Autopoiesis&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Criticism"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></div>
<p>There are multiple criticisms of the use of the term in both its original context, as an attempt to define and explain the living, and its various expanded usages, such as applying it to self-organizing systems in general or social systems in particular.<sup id="cite&#95;ref-41" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-41"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>41<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> Critics have argued that the concept and its theory fail to define or explain living systems and that, because of the extreme language of <a href="/wiki/Self-referential" class="mw-redirect" title="Self-referential">self-referentiality</a> it uses without any external reference, it is really an attempt to give substantiation to Maturana's radical <a href="/wiki/Constructivist_epistemology" class="mw-redirect" title="Constructivist epistemology">constructivist</a> or <a href="/wiki/Solipsistic" class="mw-redirect" title="Solipsistic">solipsistic</a> <a href="/wiki/Epistemology" title="Epistemology">epistemology</a>,<sup id="cite&#95;ref-42" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-42"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>42<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> or what <a href="/w/index.php?title=Danilo_Zolo&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Danilo Zolo (page does not exist)">Danilo Zolo</a><sup id="cite&#95;ref-43" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-43"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>43<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup><sup id="cite&#95;ref-Wolfe&#95;44-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Wolfe-44"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>44<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> has called instead a "desolate theology". An example is the assertion by Maturana and Varela that "We do not see what we do not see and what we do not see does not exist".<sup id="cite&#95;ref-45" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-45"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>45<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup>
</p><p>According to Razeto-Barry, the influence of <i>Autopoiesis and Cognition: The Realization of the Living</i> in mainstream biology has proven to be limited. Razeto-Barry believes that autopoiesis is not commonly used as the criterion for life.<sup id="cite&#95;ref-:2&#95;46-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:2-46"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>46<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup>
</p><p>Zoologist and philosopher <a href="/wiki/Donna_Haraway" title="Donna Haraway">Donna Haraway</a> also criticizes the usage of the term, arguing that "nothing makes itself; nothing is really autopoietic or self-organizing",<sup id="cite&#95;ref-47" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-47"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>47<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup> and suggests the use of <i>sympoiesis</i>, meaning "making-with", instead.
</p>
